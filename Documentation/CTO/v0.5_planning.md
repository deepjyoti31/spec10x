# Spec10x v0.5 Planning

> Date: March 15, 2026
> Status: Planning draft
> Goal: Define the release train after shipped v0.1, with explicit reasoning, consequences, risks, and sequencing

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Spec10x has already shipped v0.1. Today it does one thing well: a user uploads interviews, the system extracts themes, and the user can ask cited questions against their interview library.

The next product step should not be "build the whole product management operating system." That is too wide, too risky, and too slow.

The right next sequence is:

- `v0.5`: first multi-source product intelligence alpha
- `v0.51`: interview auto-sync, prioritized immediately after `v0.5`
- `v0.52`: first analytics connector plus scoring improvements
- `v0.53`: second interview connector and connector hardening if needed

The core reason for this sequence is simple:

- `v0.5` proves Spec10x can combine more than one kind of evidence
- `v0.51` solves the biggest workflow complaint from real prospects: "I do not want to upload interview files by hand"
- `v0.52` adds harder quantitative correlation only after the source model and connector machinery are proven

The most important change from the earlier draft is this:

> Interview auto-sync is now the highest-priority follow-up release after `v0.5`.

That decision is driven by direct customer signal, not by roadmap aesthetics.

## 2. Current Product Reality

Before planning the next release, it is important to be honest about what the product already is, and what it is not.

### 2.1 What v0.1 already proves

Spec10x already proves these points:

- users can upload transcripts and media
- the backend can extract insights and organize them into themes
- the app can answer cited questions over interview data
- there is a working production deployment and early beta surface

This matters because it means the next release does not need to prove "can Spec10x analyze interviews at all?" That question has already been answered.

### 2.2 What v0.1 does not prove yet

v0.1 does not yet prove these harder things:

- that Spec10x can combine multiple source types cleanly
- that the system can safely manage third-party connections over time
- that the priority score is trustworthy when it mixes different evidence types
- that users will trust a young platform with always-on access to company systems

These are not small extensions. They are a different class of product problem.

### 2.3 Technical reality that affects planning

The current codebase is already partway to a stronger architecture, but only partway.

- Ask/Q&A already uses embeddings and pgvector cosine retrieval for transcript chunks.
- Theme synthesis still uses normalized theme suggestions plus fuzzy matching, not embedding-based clustering.
- The frontend currently has lint/build, but no configured frontend automated test runner.
- The product is still mainly user-scoped, while future integrations are really company or workspace assets.

Why this matters:

- adding more UI without fixing the source foundation will create brittle product logic
- adding many connectors too early will create operational debt
- claiming "AI correlation" before the evidence model is strong will reduce trust quickly

Simple version:

- the search brain is more advanced than the clustering brain
- the product can read and answer, but it does not yet have a mature cross-source reasoning layer
- `v0.5` should fix that in a disciplined way

## 3. Planning Principles

These principles should govern every `v0.5x` decision.

### 3.1 Narrow beats broad

Decision:

- do not try to ship the whole vision in one release

Why:

- broad releases create many unfinished systems at once
- unfinished systems are expensive to maintain
- early customers forgive missing features more easily than they forgive broken trust

Consequence:

- some flashy roadmap items move out by one or two dot releases
- the release will feel smaller on paper, but safer and more believable in practice

### 3.2 Customer friction beats roadmap elegance

Decision:

- after `v0.5`, prioritize interview auto-sync over other attractive roadmap items

Why:

- customer interviews say manual upload is a real adoption blocker
- removing repeated manual work is often more valuable than adding one more analytics widget
- the product should first reduce labor, then add sophistication

Consequence:

- `v0.51` becomes a workflow win, not just a feature release
- some analytics work moves to `v0.52`

### 3.3 Trust is part of the product

Decision:

- security, permissions, storage choices, and delete/disconnect behavior are first-class product work

Why:

- for integrations, the real product is not only "insights"
- the real product is also "can I safely let this tool touch my company data?"

Consequence:

- some engineering time must go to boring but necessary systems: secret handling, audit logs, sync status, delete flows, plain-English trust docs

### 3.4 One clean model beats many special cases

Decision:

- every source should normalize into the same product-facing signal model

Why:

- otherwise each connector creates its own mini-product
- mini-products are difficult to reason about, difficult to test, and difficult to score together

Consequence:

- there is an upfront schema cost
- later features become much faster to build

## 4. Decision Log

This section captures the main decisions up front so the rest of the document is easy to read.

| Decision | Why it was chosen | Main consequence |
|---|---|---|
| `v0.5` is a narrow alpha, not the full `v0.5` vision box from the product vision doc | Shipping the full vision would combine connector work, scoring work, analytics normalization, collaboration, and UI expansion all at once | Less breadth in the first alpha, much higher chance of a real ship |
| `v0.5` focuses on support plus survey evidence, not analytics yet | Text-heavy sources reuse the current pipeline better and are easier to explain | Analytics value is delayed, but the cross-source foundation lands faster |
| `v0.51` prioritizes interview auto-sync | Real prospects do not want repeated manual upload | A highly requested workflow improvement lands early |
| Fireflies is the first interview auto-sync target | Current public docs and self-serve API-key flow are more accessible than Otter's beta/gated path | Faster first connector, but API-key handling needs careful trust messaging |
| Manual upload remains in the product as a fallback | Removing fallback would lock out unsupported sources and hurt support/debug workflows | UX should de-emphasize it after `v0.51`, not delete it |
| Storage model is minimal durable copy | Full copy creates extra trust burden; zero-copy is too slow and brittle for this product | Product stays usable while reducing unnecessary retention |
| Scoring must stay explainable | Early trust is more important than cleverness | Score v1 will be simpler but easier to defend |

## 5. Recommended Release Train

The expected path to complete the practical `v0.5` vision is:

- `v0.5`
- `v0.51`
- `v0.52`
- possibly `v0.53`

That means this is not a one-release job. It is a release family.

### 5.1 v0.5 - Multi-Source Evidence Alpha

User promise:

> "Spec10x can combine interviews with other customer evidence and show what matters in one place."

What it must ship:

- source foundation
- one support-source connector
- survey/NPS CSV import
- unified feed as a separate `/feed` route
- source-aware themes
- impact score v1
- evidence-first priority board in Sprint 6 after the score engine is live
- source health and sync visibility in Sprint 6 operational follow-on work

What it should ship if capacity allows:

- saved views
- collections
- theme merge

What it should explicitly not ship:

- analytics connector
- second or third support provider
- broad collaboration suite
- complex revenue attribution
- real-time webhook fan-out across many sources

Why this release exists:

- it proves the product can move beyond single-source interviews
- it lays the shared schema and connector machinery needed by later releases
- it gives users a visible new value proposition, not just backend work

Main consequence:

- `v0.5` is more foundation-heavy than flashy
- that is acceptable because `v0.51` can then move quickly

Implementation update as of March 19, 2026:

- Sprint 4 and Sprint 5 scope is complete in the codebase
- non-interview theme matching is deterministic for `v0.5` and persists the primary match in `signals.metadata_json.theme_match`
- survey CSV import is repeatable and remains available as an import workflow, not a one-time connected source slot
- Impact Score v1 now uses the balanced formula: frequency `40`, negative sentiment `25`, recency `20`, source diversity `15`
- the priority board, trust-copy package, and observability review surface are still Sprint 6 work and are not part of the accepted Sprint 4 and Sprint 5 scope

### 5.2 v0.51 - Automatic Interview Sync

User promise:

> "Connect once, and Spec10x keeps your interview library up to date automatically."

What it must ship:

- first interview auto-sync connector
- integration settings page for that connector
- first historical backfill
- incremental sync
- sync status, retry, disconnect
- manual upload preserved as fallback

Why this release exists:

- customer feedback says manual upload is real friction
- auto-sync is a habit-forming feature, not just a convenience feature
- it converts Spec10x from "tool you remember to feed" into "tool that keeps itself current"

Main consequence:

- the product becomes much easier to adopt for teams already using meeting tools
- trust, permission clarity, and sync reliability become much more important

### 5.3 v0.52 - Analytics and Scoring v2

User promise:

> "Spec10x can now connect product behavior with customer language."

What it should ship:

- first analytics connector
- score v2 with better breakdowns
- simple trends over time
- stronger evidence panels and source filters

Why this is later:

- analytics data is harder to normalize than text
- analytics correlations are easier to overstate and mislead
- once the connector platform and trust model exist, analytics becomes safer to add

Main consequence:

- analytics lands later, but on top of a more reliable base

### 5.4 v0.53 - Second Interview Connector and Hardening

User promise:

> "Spec10x supports more than one interview source, and integrations feel stable."

What it should ship if needed:

- second interview connector
- connector reliability improvements
- better admin controls
- possible lightweight collaboration if pilots still demand it

Why this may be needed:

- some of the original `v0.5` vision likely spills over naturally
- a second connector proves the architecture is reusable, not custom-built for one provider

Main consequence:

- this release may not be necessary if `v0.52` is small and `v0.51` leaves enough room
- but planning for it is more honest than pretending everything fits into one step

## 6. Detailed Scope for v0.5

This is the core release. It needs to be detailed enough to guide implementation, not just inspire it.

### 6.1 Source Foundation

Decision:

- build the source model first, before adding too many UI surfaces

Why this decision was taken:

- without a shared source model, every connector becomes special-case code
- special-case code feels faster in week one and becomes painful in month two
- the source model is the skeleton of the entire multi-source product

What needs to be added:

- `data_sources`
- `source_connections`
- `sync_runs`
- `source_items`
- `signals`

What each table is for, in plain English:

- `data_sources`: the catalog of source types and providers, for example `support/zendesk`, `interview/fireflies`, `survey/csv`
- `source_connections`: one customer's connection to one provider
- `sync_runs`: the logbook of every sync attempt
- `source_items`: the mapping from upstream object IDs to internal objects, so we do not duplicate or lose track of records
- `signals`: the normalized product-level evidence rows that power feeds, themes, scores, and filters

Why these particular tables were chosen:

- they separate "how we got the data" from "how we use the data"
- they let us answer hard debugging questions later:
  - what provider was connected?
  - when did the last sync run?
  - which upstream record produced this signal?
  - did this signal already exist or was it duplicated?

Consequences of this decision:

- more backend work happens before the most impressive UI work
- migrations and schema design take real time
- later connectors are much easier because they plug into a stable shape

Risks:

- over-designing the schema before real usage
- turning the foundation into a mini platform project

Mitigation:

- keep the model minimal
- optimize for the next 2 to 4 connectors, not the next 40

### 6.2 One Support Connector

Decision:

- ship one real support-source connector in `v0.5`

Why this decision was taken:

- a real connector proves the connector architecture works in production
- support tickets are closer to interviews than analytics events are
- they are mostly text, so the existing extraction and theme pipeline can be reused

Why not many support connectors:

- provider count multiplies maintenance work quickly
- each vendor has different auth, pagination, rate limits, and payload shape

Consequence:

- `v0.5` proves the architecture with one live connector instead of pretending broad coverage
- some prospects on other support tools will need to wait

Risk:

- picking the wrong vendor first

Mitigation:

- choose based on current pilot demand and public API accessibility, not completeness

### 6.3 Survey/NPS CSV Import

Decision:

- ship survey/NPS CSV import in `v0.5`

Why this decision was taken:

- CSV import is lower engineering risk than another live connector
- it still gives multi-source breadth
- it is lower trust friction for cautious customers

Consequences:

- survey data can be used in the product quickly
- the import path is not as elegant as a live integration

Why this is still valuable:

- the user's real problem is "use my evidence," not "only use it if it came through OAuth"
- CSV is a valid stepping stone when trust and speed both matter

### 6.4 Unified Feed

Decision:

- ship a single feed where interviews, support signals, and survey signals can be viewed together

Why this decision was taken:

- users need to feel the product shift from single-source to multi-source
- a unified feed is the clearest visual proof that the model works

What the feed should show:

- source type
- timestamp
- title or short summary
- sentiment or signal type
- link back to original evidence
- theme association if already assigned

Consequences:

- the UI becomes more obviously useful
- the normalization work must be good enough that mixed-source rows still feel coherent

Risk:

- a messy feed that mixes incompatible things without clear labels

Mitigation:

- strong badges, filters, and consistent row structure

### 6.5 Source-Aware Themes

Decision:

- themes must show evidence by source type, not just a single combined blob

Why this decision was taken:

- a PM needs to know whether a theme is supported by interviews, tickets, surveys, or all of them
- source diversity is part of the signal strength

Consequences:

- theme detail pages become more informative and more trustworthy
- the data model needs source metadata attached to every signal

Risk:

- users could wrongly interpret "many sources" as proof of causation

Mitigation:

- make the UI say "supporting evidence from X sources," not "confirmed root cause"

### 6.6 Impact Score v1

Decision:

- score v1 should be simple and explainable

Recommended first inputs:

- frequency
- recency
- negative sentiment weight
- source diversity

Why this decision was taken:

- early scoring should help prioritization, not act mysterious
- if users cannot understand the score, they will not trust it
- simple scores are easier to debug when they look wrong

Why not revenue impact and advanced segmentation yet:

- those depend on stronger data models and better downstream integrations
- fake precision is worse than simple honesty

Consequences:

- score v1 will be less "smart" than the long-term vision
- score v1 will be much easier to defend in front of a skeptical customer

Risk:

- users may expect the score to be more magical than it is

Mitigation:

- show the score breakdown directly in the UI

### 6.7 Priority Board

Decision:

- ship a priority board in `v0.5`, but make it evidence-first, not workflow-heavy

Why this decision was taken:

- users need a decision surface, not only a feed
- a board makes the multi-source insight actionable

What the first version should be:

- automatically ranked by score
- source breakdown visible on each card
- trend and evidence preview visible
- lightweight manual override allowed

What it should not try to be yet:

- a full project management board
- a task tracker
- a large collaboration layer with threaded comments and complex assignment logic

Consequences:

- the board stays focused on prioritization
- some customers will still need Jira or Linear for execution tracking, which is fine

### 6.8 Collections, Theme Merge, Saved Views

Decision:

- treat these as support features for `v0.5`, not the headline

Why this decision was taken:

- they improve usability and reduce v0.1 rough edges
- they are not the main strategic bet

Consequence:

- ship them if capacity allows, but do not let them delay the core multi-source wedge

## 7. Detailed Scope for v0.51

This release matters because it is directly driven by customer demand.

### 7.1 Core Product Goal

Decision:

- make interview auto-sync the primary workflow for supported providers

Why this decision was taken:

- manual upload adds repeated user work
- repeated user work kills habit products
- customers with existing note-taker tools already expect their interviews to be somewhere digitally accessible

Simple version:

- if the user has to keep remembering to feed the app, the app feels like homework
- if the app stays current by itself, it feels like infrastructure

### 7.2 First Provider Choice: Fireflies

Decision:

- build Fireflies first

Why this decision was taken:

- current official Fireflies docs expose a public API path
- current official Fireflies help docs show a self-serve API-key access flow
- this is a lower-friction starting point than Otter's current public API posture

What this means:

- Fireflies is not necessarily the biggest provider in every market
- it is the best first engineering target because it reduces time-to-first-auto-sync

Consequences:

- faster first connector
- we inherit API-key handling tradeoffs instead of OAuth tradeoffs

### 7.3 Why Not Otter First

Decision:

- do not build Otter first unless its API access becomes practically easier

Why this decision was taken:

- current official Otter help states the public API is in beta and starts through an account manager
- that adds business friction before technical work is even complete

Consequences:

- Otter users may have to wait for a later release
- this is acceptable because the first goal is a reliable first auto-sync path, not maximum provider coverage

### 7.4 Auto-Sync UX

The user flow should be:

1. Open Integrations
2. Choose Fireflies
3. Add credentials
4. Validate connection
5. Start historical backfill
6. Watch sync status
7. See imported interviews appear automatically

Why this flow matters:

- it turns setup into a one-time event
- it gives visible trust checkpoints
- it avoids the feeling that data disappears into a black box

### 7.5 Why Manual Upload Must Still Exist

Decision:

- keep manual upload as a fallback, but stop treating it as the hero path for supported providers

Why this decision was taken:

- unsupported providers still need an entry path
- users may want to import historical exports or one-off transcripts
- fallback paths are useful for debugging and sales demos

Important nuance:

- keeping fallback does not mean marketing it as the main workflow
- after `v0.51`, the product copy should lead with auto-sync where supported

Consequence:

- the product stays flexible without ignoring customer demand

## 8. Connector Architecture

This section explains how the connector system should work.

### 8.1 Mental Model

Think of a connector as a small translator robot.

Its job is:

1. get permission
2. fetch data
3. convert it into Spec10x's common format
4. remember where it left off
5. come back later without creating duplicates

That is all.

If a connector starts doing product logic, scoring logic, or UI logic, the architecture is going wrong.

### 8.2 Connector Contract

Recommended interface:

- `connect()`
- `validate()`
- `backfill()`
- `sync_incremental()`
- `normalize()`
- `disconnect()`

What each method means:

- `connect()`: create the connection record and store credential references
- `validate()`: test that credentials and scopes are usable
- `backfill()`: import historical records
- `sync_incremental()`: fetch only new or changed records since the last cursor
- `normalize()`: convert provider payloads into the shared `signals` shape
- `disconnect()`: stop future syncs and remove secret references

Why a contract matters:

- every provider has different details
- the platform should still know what a connector is supposed to do

Consequence:

- new connectors become smaller and more predictable

### 8.3 Storage and Sync Lifecycle

The lifecycle should be:

1. create connection
2. validate
3. store secret reference
4. create first `sync_run`
5. backfill upstream items
6. upsert `source_items`
7. normalize into `signals`
8. record status and cursor
9. run scheduled incremental sync later

Why this lifecycle was chosen:

- it separates connection state from sync state
- it makes failures debuggable
- it allows safe retries

### 8.4 Idempotency

Decision:

- every sync must be idempotent

Plain-English meaning:

- if the same sync runs twice, it should not create duplicate rows or corrupt data

Why this decision was taken:

- background jobs fail in the real world
- networks time out
- providers send the same data more than once

Consequence:

- `source_items` must track upstream IDs and update markers
- upserts matter more than inserts

### 8.5 Normalized Signal Shape

Every signal should have a core shared shape, even if some fields are optional.

Recommended fields:

- `id`
- `user_id` or future `workspace_id`
- `source_connection_id`
- `source_type`
- `provider`
- `external_id`
- `occurred_at`
- `content_text`
- `title`
- `author_or_speaker`
- `sentiment`
- `metadata_json`
- `source_url`
- `status`

Why this shape was chosen:

- it gives enough common structure for feeds, filters, themes, and evidence panels
- it avoids hard-coding every provider's schema into product logic

Consequence:

- provider-specific richness lives in `metadata_json` or separate provider-specific tables
- the core product stays source-agnostic

### 8.6 Ownership Model Risk

Important architectural warning:

- integrations are really organization assets, not purely personal-user assets

Current reality:

- the product is mostly user-scoped today

Why this matters:

- if one PM connects a company-wide source, the ownership and delete model becomes more sensitive

Recommended approach:

- if full workspaces are not added in `v0.5`, design new source tables so a future workspace migration is possible without major rewrites

Consequence:

- this adds some design caution now
- it avoids a painful migration later

## 9. Security and Trust Model

This section matters because customer fear about connecting data is rational.

### 9.1 Core Principle

Decision:

- ask for the smallest amount of access and store the smallest amount of data that still makes the product good

Why this decision was taken:

- large trust asks are hard for a new company
- storing unnecessary data increases legal, security, and reputational risk

### 9.2 Storage Model: Minimal Durable Copy

Decision:

- use minimal durable copy

What we should store:

- transcript text
- timestamps
- speaker or participant metadata if needed
- normalized signals
- embeddings
- source IDs and source links
- sync cursors and status

What we should not store by default:

- raw audio or video from third-party providers unless there is a strong product need
- giant full provider payloads forever
- unused metadata just because the provider returns it
- secrets in normal database fields

Why this model was chosen:

- full-copy storage is easy for engineering but heavy on trust
- zero-copy analysis sounds safer but is usually too slow, too rate-limited, and too fragile

Simple version:

- do not keep the whole warehouse if you only need the shelf labels and the product boxes

Consequences:

- product performance stays good
- long-term analysis stays possible
- trust burden stays lower than "store everything"

### 9.3 Why Zero-Copy Is Not the Main Plan

This question comes up naturally: can Spec10x leave the data in the source system and analyze it there?

Technically, sometimes yes.

Practically, for this product, not as the main model.

Why not:

- live fetches are slower
- APIs rate-limit
- historical comparisons become harder
- source systems may not keep the shape we need forever
- cross-source scoring becomes expensive and brittle

Consequence:

- zero-copy can exist as a narrow future mode for some enterprise cases
- it should not be the default product architecture

### 9.4 Permission Model

Decision:

- read-only where possible, least privilege always

Why this decision was taken:

- Spec10x is an analysis product, not a writeback product
- write scopes increase risk and scare customers

Consequence:

- no provider writeback in early connector releases
- the product cannot, for example, modify tickets or delete provider content, which is good

### 9.5 Secret Handling

Decision:

- all provider credentials go to Secret Manager, not to ordinary app tables

Why this decision was taken:

- secrets are the most sensitive part of connector infrastructure
- accidental exposure here is much worse than a normal application bug

Consequence:

- connection rows should store references, not raw tokens or keys

### 9.6 Disconnect and Delete

Decision:

- every connector must support clear disconnect and imported-data delete behavior

Why this decision was taken:

- customers need confidence that they can leave cleanly
- "easy to leave" increases willingness to try

Important nuance for API-key integrations:

- deleting the stored key on our side stops future syncs
- the customer may still need to rotate or revoke the key in the provider system itself

Consequence:

- the UI must explain this clearly

### 9.7 Trust Package

Before or alongside `v0.51`, prepare these customer-facing assets:

- one-page security overview
- plain-English explanation of each permission requested
- clear "we do not train on your data" statement
- subprocessor list
- delete/disconnect documentation
- privacy and terms language that matches real behavior

Why this work matters:

- a young company cannot rely on brand trust
- clear documentation reduces "unknown platform" fear

## 10. Provider Strategy for Interview Auto-Sync

### 10.1 Fireflies First

As of March 15, 2026, the current recommendation is:

- build Fireflies first

Why:

- official public API documentation is available
- official help content describes API-key access
- this appears easier to start with than providers whose public APIs are still gated or beta-only

Tradeoff:

- API-key setup is simpler to build than OAuth
- API-key setup is usually less elegant from a security and revocation standpoint

Consequence:

- faster first auto-sync release
- stronger need for Secret Manager, clear setup docs, and explicit disconnect instructions

### 10.2 Otter Later

As of March 15, 2026, the current recommendation is:

- do not make Otter the first connector

Why:

- current official help describes the public API as beta and routed through account-manager access

Consequence:

- Otter becomes a later connector candidate, not the first implementation target

### 10.3 What Could Change This Decision

This recommendation should be revisited if any of these become true:

- Otter opens a self-serve public API path
- pilot demand is overwhelmingly concentrated on Otter
- Fireflies access patterns become meaningfully more restrictive

That means the provider choice is evidence-based, not ideological.

## 11. Risks and Mitigations

This section is intentionally written in plain language.

### 11.1 False Correlation

Simple explanation:

- two things happen at the same time and the product wrongly suggests one explains the other

Example:

- support tickets complain about search
- usage also drops on the search page
- the system suggests one clean story
- but maybe the drop came from a separate outage or experiment

Why this is dangerous:

- the product sounds smart but is wrong
- once users catch a few wrong stories, trust falls fast

Mitigation:

- do not claim causation when we only have supporting evidence
- show the score inputs and source evidence
- use phrases like "supported by" and "related signals," not "proven cause"

Residual risk:

- users may still over-interpret summaries, so wording matters a lot

### 11.2 Source Explosion

Simple explanation:

- too many connectors too early turns the roadmap into connector maintenance

Why this is dangerous:

- every provider has quirks
- small quirks add up into slow teams and fragile releases

Mitigation:

- one provider per source category first
- do not add provider number two until provider number one is stable

Residual risk:

- sales pressure may push for more logos before the architecture is ready

### 11.3 Weak Common Data Model

Simple explanation:

- if each new source lands in the database differently, the rest of the product becomes spaghetti

Why this is dangerous:

- every feed, score, filter, and theme page becomes custom logic

Mitigation:

- define `signals` before building many connectors
- keep provider weirdness inside connector modules

Residual risk:

- the first version of the model may still need revision after real usage

### 11.4 Ownership Model Mismatch

Simple explanation:

- company data may get tied too tightly to one user account

Why this is dangerous:

- if one person leaves the company or deletes their account, integration ownership becomes confusing

Mitigation:

- plan source ownership so a workspace model can be added cleanly
- avoid assuming integrations are purely personal forever

Residual risk:

- this may remain awkward until full team/workspace support exists

### 11.5 Operational Sync Failures

Simple explanation:

- the robot bringing data back gets tired, lost, or interrupted

What can go wrong:

- bad credentials
- expired keys
- rate limits
- partial imports
- duplicates
- provider outages

Why this is dangerous:

- users only see "the connector is flaky"

Mitigation:

- retries
- idempotent upserts
- visible sync status
- `sync_runs` logs
- manual re-sync
- cursor tracking

Residual risk:

- some providers are still simply messy, so support load will rise

### 11.6 Security and Trust Failure

Simple explanation:

- one mistake with company data can damage trust far beyond one customer

Why this is dangerous:

- early-stage companies do not get many chances here

Mitigation:

- least privilege
- read-only use
- Secret Manager
- encryption
- audit logs
- delete/disconnect controls
- plain-English trust materials

Residual risk:

- some customers still will not connect anything until the company is older, which is normal

### 11.7 Testing and Observability Gap

Simple explanation:

- more moving parts means more silent failures

Why this is dangerous:

- a broken sync can go unnoticed until a customer complains

Mitigation:

- connector contract tests
- replay tests with saved provider responses
- sync lifecycle tests
- frontend smoke coverage for integration UI
- alerts on repeated sync failures

Residual risk:

- provider changes can still break assumptions unexpectedly

### 11.8 Cost and Rate-Limit Surprise

Simple explanation:

- connectors can create unexpected cloud bills and provider-throttling problems

Why this is dangerous:

- large backfills and repeated polling can get expensive
- slow syncs can make the product feel broken

Mitigation:

- backfill limits
- polling intervals tuned per provider
- cursor-based incremental sync
- metrics on job duration, sync size, and API failure rate

Residual risk:

- some customers will still have much larger histories than expected

- sync success rate
- sync failure rate by provider

- duplicate import rate

At minimum, create simple internal runbooks for:

- invalid credentials
- stuck sync
- provider outage
- duplicate import repair
- disconnect/delete requests

Why this matters:

- runbooks turn panic into procedure

## 13. Success Metrics and Decision Gates

### 13.1 v0.5 Success

`v0.5` is successful if:

- users can combine interviews with at least one live support source and one survey import path
- unified feed usage is real
- theme pages clearly show multi-source support
- users understand the impact score
- the priority board helps answer "what should we investigate next?"

Decision gate:

- if users do not trust the score or do not use the board, do not rush into more connectors
- fix evidence presentation first

### 13.2 v0.51 Success

`v0.51` is successful if:

- a supported interview source can connect once and stay current
- backfill completes reliably
- incremental sync behaves predictably
- customers prefer the auto-sync path over manual upload

Decision gate:

- if adoption is low because setup feels scary, improve trust and onboarding before adding provider number two

### 13.3 v0.52 Success

`v0.52` is successful if:

- analytics adds useful signal without confusing the product
- users can see why a trend or score changed
- correlations remain evidence-backed and inspectable

Decision gate:

- if analytics makes the product noisier rather than clearer, simplify before expanding

## 15. Final Recommendation

The right strategy is not "ship everything in `v0.5`."

The right strategy is:

- make `v0.5` the first credible multi-source evidence release
- make `v0.51` the workflow release that removes manual interview upload pain
- keep the architecture, trust model, and score explainability strong enough that later analytics work does not collapse under its own weight

In short:

> `v0.5` should prove Spec10x can combine evidence.
> `v0.51` should prove Spec10x can stay current automatically.
> `v0.52` should prove Spec10x can connect customer language to product behavior without losing trust.

That is a believable, customer-driven release train.
